Sony Network Entertainment International

May 2015 - August 2015

Introduction

Sony Network Entertainment International (SNEI) develops the online network infrastructure for Sony to provide top-quality entertainment around the globe through games, videos, music, and other services. I worked at the Waterloo division, led by Tony Jedlovsky, on the design team under my supervisor, Jesse Wong.

The Waterloo division of SNEI is responsible for software engineering and design of various SNEI services such as the online PlayStation store, MediaGo, and the PS Video App. As a user experience designer, I was mainly responsible for evaluating and improving the user experience of the PlayStation web store. I would like to thank Jesse Wong for being a great mentor and supervisor, as well as Wynn Yau for being a wonderful fellow design co-op student to work with.



UX has Feelings, Too

The main requirement for the PlayStation store is that it must have the basic functions of an e-commerce website: the ability to browse, search, and purchase products. The store must also be responsive, or be able to render itself for proper viewing and interaction on mobile devices, which have significantly smaller screen space than a desktop computer.

As part of a design overhaul project, the design team decided to ask various employees at the SNEI Waterloo location on what they thought the overall user experience of the PlayStation store should be using keywords that the design team came up with while thinking about design heuristics as well as the overall PlayStation brand. Some key words were also added in by the employees during the exercise. In the exercise, the employees were asked to sort the words into three tiers of importance, with the 1st tier being the most important. In the following table, the user experience keywords are shown in order of their overall score.



The overall score was calculated by giving three points every time it was sorted into 1st tier, two points every time it was sorted into 2nd tier, and one point every time it was sorted into 3rd tier, then dividing up that total by the number of employees that participated (nine, in this case.) In other words, if a key word was sorted into 1st tier by all of the participants, its overall score would be 3.

As seen, some obvious user experience key words are at the top. An e-commerce store most certainly should be easy to use and organized, and should perform fast to allow for the user to get the product they want as quickly as possible. Other, less technical terms also came up, which the design team felt was important to keep in mind, since users also want to feel certain ways about the overall store based on its aesthetics and user experience choices, such as being seamless and engaging. These also fit in with the PlayStation brand, as many modern video games are becoming more and more engaging, realistic, and seamless. Other design requirements are that the mobile website should include familiar mobile gesture like scrolling down, offer menus for specific navigations, offer an option use the full site, as well as keeping high-level, important functions visible at the top.

To remind ourselves of the keywords that we came up with throughout the design process, Wynn created a lovely wordle with the most important keywords having the most prominence.

Manulife RED Lab

September 2014 - December 2014



Towards the end of my four months at the Manulife Research, Exploration, and Development (RED) Lab, our team helped run the ZOOM Day Challenge - a program designed for local high school students to experience first-hand real-life work. We guided three high school students through a rapid prototyping challenge of making an app that would improve the banking experience. At the beginning, I wasn’t sure where they take our challenge, but in the end I was impressed. After going through a brainstorming exercise, many design iterations and customer interviews, they presented an interactive prototype to encourage saving. They had fun and came out in the end a little more knowledge about iterative mobile app design.

Upon reflection, their experience over the course of a day was almost like what I went through the four months of my co-op. Not knowing a lot at the beginning, but eager to learn, and coming out with a lot more knowledge and experience.

Overall, my time at the RED Lab was amazing. I got to be in an environment of fun, knowledgable, generally awesome techie people who could really appreciate a good start-up-related joke. (“That’s disruptive!”) I worked on projects focused mostly on user interaction innovation on web and iOS platforms. And even though before this work term I’ve never even owned a Mac before, it was a really cool opportunity to be able to learn Swift and Objective-C. Because of it, I was able to used those skills learned and actually won the best iOS app prize at my school’s hackathon! Our team also won the co-op innovation challenge for our facial-recognition idea for Manulife security, which was a fun side project.

I’ve learned a lot of technical skills this term, but perhaps the most important thing I will be taking away is the spirit of innovation and prototyping. It’s the combination of collaborative and individual work that drives this spirit, and I look forward to implementing this where needed wherever I go in the future.

FarmTable

Side Project.

Through a referral, I was asked by the founder of FarmTable to do a quick visual mockup of how the landing page could possibly look. I ended up having time to create a simple layout with the general components.

I first started out by asking about the market research the founder had already done in Alberta, and what the farmers and restaurant's needs and frustrations were. In summary, the website needed to look professional, polished, and give them the information they want. Also, the average farmer would not have high technological familiarity, so I wanted to make sure that it was clear what the main function of FarmTable was from the get-go. This led to the idea of visually showing that FarmTable was the bridge between farmers and restaurants, which was included early on in the design process.

After some wireframes were drawn out, a quick draft was made in Sketch. In the design, I tried to follow Norman/Nielsen design principles to help the user match the website to their mental model of an e-commerce site. I included example items that the user could order right now order items so that it's clear it's an e-commerce website. This also serves as another entry point for onboarding, as users who would click the clear call-to-action of "Add to Cart" would be taken to a modal or new page to sign-in or register. Further down the page, I created an area to easily sign-up, with visual icons for different types of farms (something I learned about the farming users from interviewing the founder). Overall, I tried to make the design more visual, have minimal text, and straight-forward sign-up to communicate the purpose of FarmTable.

If I could go back to improve this project, I would try to map out some of the user flows more and create specificationss for exactly what each interaction item would lead to - this would also lead to the need of creating higher fidelity prototypes, since new interactions are probably needed (e.g. selecting the number of carrots on the example carrot item). I would also like to have designed more screens, such as how the sign-up would have looked like for the restaurant managers. I would also fix the visual unpleasantries - for example, the colour of "farmers" and the background image behind it were simply placeholders, as most of the images and text in the mock-up were.

Tools used: Sketch.

Judio (formerly "Jam")

Personal project.

Judio is a social Music-Making App. Users can record their instrument or singing, then send it to their friends. They can then record and layer their own singing/instrument on top of it, and send it out again, etc. to create multi-track music. This makes long-distance music-making possible.

I conceived the idea as a result of a personal frustration - a high school friend and I loved to sing together, but we ended up going to different universities. We tried to create multi-track recordings together through various methods: by sending files back and forth through Audacity, by singing through Skype, etc. Nothing worked well for us. Thus, the idea for Judio was born.

The first iteration was developed as Android app at MHacks Hackathon January 2013. I worked on it as a developer, focusing on Android AudioStream libraries and functions to multi-layer tracks. The project was further developed while I at the Velocity Residence in Summer 2014. I lived with 70 other selected students who were passionate about entreprenuership. Each week, we were given talks by the start-up experienced to help further our ideas. Judio ended up being a finalist for $1k Velocity Fund Final pitch competition.

In September 2014, the idea was brought to Hack the North, UW's inaugural hackathon, and Canada's biggest. As part of a team of three, I coded the UI in iOS using Swift, and coded the multi-track layering and playback using both Swift and Objective C. Our project ended up being the winner of the Best iOS App prize sponsored by Apple. The project is currently on hold.

Tools used: iOS/Swift/Obj-C. Paint.NET. Android/Java.
